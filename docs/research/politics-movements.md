# Politics & Movements in 1820s Ireland

## Research Document — Parish (Kilteevan, County Roscommon, 1820s)

### The Shadow of the Act of Union

The political landscape of 1820s Ireland was defined above all by the **Act of Union of 1800**, which dissolved the **Irish Parliament** in Dublin and merged the Kingdom of Ireland into the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland. What had been a semi-independent legislature — however imperfect, however dominated by the **Protestant Ascendancy** — was now gone. Ireland sent roughly one hundred members to the **House of Commons at Westminster** and a number of representative peers to the House of Lords, but effective political power had shifted decisively across the Irish Sea. For the majority Catholic population, the Union was experienced as a deepening of colonial subordination. William Pitt the Younger had implied that Catholic emancipation would follow the Union as a concession, but **King George III** blocked any such measure, and the promise went unfulfilled for nearly three decades. In parishes like Kilteevan, the practical consequence was that local power remained firmly in the hands of the **Protestant landlord class**, who controlled not only the land but the levers of political representation.

### The Protestant Ascendancy and Electoral Control

Parliamentary representation in County Roscommon, as across rural Ireland, was dominated by the **Protestant Ascendancy** — the Anglo-Irish landed gentry who held vast estates, controlled borough seats, and treated elections as extensions of their property rights. The franchise in county seats was held by **forty-shilling freeholders**, men who held freehold land valued at forty shillings or more per year. In practice, many of these voters were Catholic tenants who qualified through long leases granted by their landlords. They could vote, but no Catholic could sit in Parliament — the **Penal Laws**, though much eroded by the Relief Acts of 1778 and 1793, still barred Catholics from membership in the Commons and Lords, from holding senior offices of state, and from the higher ranks of the judiciary. The forty-shilling freeholder was thus in a peculiar position: enfranchised but not truly represented, and subject to intense pressure from his landlord regarding how to cast his vote.

**Landlord control of elections** was near-total in many constituencies. Voting was conducted openly — there was no secret ballot — and a tenant who defied his landlord's wishes at the hustings risked eviction, rent increases, or social ruin. Landlords regarded their tenants' votes as a species of property, an extension of the deference owed to the estate. The **grand jury system**, which governed local taxation and public works at the county level, was likewise a creature of the Ascendancy, its members drawn exclusively from the propertied Protestant elite. For ordinary Catholics in a parish like Kilteevan, formal politics was something that happened above and around them, shaped by distant powers and local magnates alike.

### Daniel O'Connell and the Catholic Association

Into this landscape stepped **Daniel O'Connell**, the Kerry-born barrister who would become the most consequential Irish political figure of the nineteenth century. O'Connell was a Catholic, a landowner, a gifted orator, and a constitutional radical — committed to achieving political change through legal and peaceful means rather than armed rebellion. In **1823**, he founded the **Catholic Association**, an organization devoted to securing **Catholic emancipation**: the right of Catholics to sit in Parliament and hold the full range of public offices. What made the Catholic Association revolutionary was not its goal, which had been pursued by genteel Catholic committees for decades, but its method of mass mobilization.

The key innovation was the **penny rent** (later called the **Catholic rent**), a subscription of one penny per month collected from ordinary Catholics across Ireland. This transformed the emancipation campaign from an elite lobbying effort into a genuinely popular movement. Parish priests became the primary collectors and organizers, gathering pennies after Sunday Mass and channeling them upward through a network that reached every corner of the country. In Roscommon, as elsewhere, the **Catholic chapel** became a site of political organization as much as worship. The sums raised were enormous in aggregate, funding legal challenges, propaganda, and the Association's administrative apparatus. More importantly, the act of contributing gave the poorest tenant a stake in the campaign and a sense of collective political agency that had no precedent in Irish life.

O'Connell's movement built steadily through the mid-1820s, holding vast **monster meetings** (though that term became more common in the 1840s) and rallying public opinion. The British government attempted to suppress the Catholic Association in 1825, but O'Connell simply dissolved it and reconstituted it under a new name, exploiting legal technicalities with a lawyer's precision. By the late 1820s, the movement had become an irresistible force.

### The Clare By-Election of 1828

The crisis came to a head with the **Clare by-election of 1828**, a watershed moment in Irish and British political history. When **Vesey Fitzgerald**, a popular and relatively liberal Protestant landlord, was appointed to the Cabinet and required to seek re-election in County Clare, O'Connell stood against him — knowing that as a Catholic he could not legally take his seat if elected. The campaign electrified Ireland. Catholic forty-shilling freeholders, organized by their priests and emboldened by the Association, defied their landlords en masse and voted for O'Connell. He won decisively. The result confronted the British government with an impossible dilemma: refuse to seat O'Connell and face potential insurrection, or concede emancipation. The **Duke of Wellington** and **Robert Peel**, both opponents of emancipation, concluded that concession was the lesser danger. The **Roman Catholic Relief Act of 1829** followed, removing the major civil disabilities against Catholics. As a punitive counterweight, the franchise qualification was raised from forty shillings to ten pounds, disenfranchising the very tenants who had delivered the victory — a bitter irony that was not lost on anyone.

### Orange Order and Ribbon Lodges

Political life in 1820s Ireland was also shaped by **sectarian organizations** that operated below the level of parliamentary politics. The **Orange Order**, founded in 1795 in County Armagh after the sectarian **Battle of the Diamond**, was a Protestant fraternal organization dedicated to defending the Protestant constitution and the memory of **William of Orange**. Its annual **Twelfth of July marches**, commemorating William's victory at the Battle of the Boyne in 1690, were deliberate assertions of Protestant supremacy and frequent flashpoints for violence. The Orange Order was strongest in Ulster but had lodges across Ireland, including in Connacht, and its members included landlords, magistrates, yeomanry officers, and ordinary Protestant tradesmen. In mixed communities, the Order's marches and gatherings were sources of deep anxiety for the Catholic population.

The Catholic counterpart — though never as formally organized — were the **Ribbon lodges** (or **Ribbonmen**), secret oath-bound societies that emerged in the early nineteenth century as successors to the **Defender** tradition. Ribbonmen were concerned with local grievances — tithes, rents, evictions, sectarian attacks — and operated through intimidation, boycott, and occasional violence. They were strongest among the Catholic poor and were viewed with suspicion by both the Catholic clergy (who condemned secret oaths) and the emancipation movement (which feared their radicalism would discredit the constitutional campaign). In a parish like Kilteevan, whispered Ribbon affiliations might attach to certain families or townlands, a source of tension that the priest and the political organizers would struggle to manage.

### Local Power Brokers

At the parish level, political influence was concentrated in three figures: **the priest**, **the landlord**, and **the publican**. The Catholic priest was the natural leader of his flock, the most educated man in the community, and — through the Catholic Association — increasingly a political organizer. His authority was both spiritual and temporal, and his endorsement or condemnation could make or break a man's standing. The Protestant landlord controlled the land, the leases, access to employment, and the machinery of local government. His agent — often more feared than the landlord himself — managed the day-to-day exercise of this power. The publican, who kept the local **shebeen** or licensed house, was a broker of information, credit, and social connection, a man who heard everything and whose premises served as an informal meeting place for political discussion. These three figures existed in a complex web of cooperation, rivalry, and mutual dependence that defined the political texture of rural life.

### The Memory of 1798 and the United Irishmen

The 1820s were close enough to the **Rebellion of 1798** that its memory remained vivid and politically potent. The **United Irishmen**, inspired by the French and American revolutions, had attempted to unite Catholic and Protestant in a republican uprising against British rule. The rebellion was crushed with extreme brutality — mass executions, transportation, the burning of homes, and military terror that left deep scars on the collective memory. In Connacht, the short-lived **French landing at Killala** in 1798 and the subsequent rout of the Franco-Irish force at **Ballinamuck** in County Longford — not far from Roscommon — would have been within living memory for older parishioners in the 1820s. Veterans and survivors, or their children, carried stories of those weeks of hope and catastrophe.

**Robert Emmet's** abortive rising in Dublin in **1803**, and his famous speech from the dock before his execution, added another layer to the republican martyrology. Emmet's call to let no man write his epitaph until Ireland took her place among the nations of the earth became one of the most quoted passages in Irish political rhetoric. For O'Connell and the constitutional movement, the lesson of 1798 and 1803 was clear: armed rebellion led to disaster, and only peaceful, legal agitation could succeed. But for others — particularly the young, the desperate, and those touched by Ribbon sentiment — the memory of '98 carried a different charge: the possibility, however remote, that Ireland might yet be freed by force.

**Liberal Protestantism** also played a role in the political ferment of the period. Not all Protestants were Orangemen or defenders of the Ascendancy. A strand of **reform-minded Protestant opinion**, rooted in Enlightenment values and the legacy of the United Irishmen themselves (many of whose leaders were Presbyterians and Anglicans), supported Catholic emancipation and broader parliamentary reform. These voices were a minority, but they complicated the simple sectarian binary and offered the possibility of cross-community alliance.

### Game Design Implications

The political dynamics of 1820s Ireland offer rich material for *Parish*. **NPC dialogue** should reflect the pervasive political tensions of the era: Catholic characters might speak with guarded hope about O'Connell's campaign, debate the merits of the penny rent, or express anxiety about landlord retaliation at election time. Protestant characters might range from rigid Orangemen to sympathetic reformers, with the majority occupying an uneasy middle ground. **Daniel O'Connell** himself should function as a powerful **offscreen figure** — discussed, admired, feared, quoted, but never directly encountered — whose campaign provides a background arc of rising tension across the game's timeline.

**Faction allegiances** can be modeled through the competing pulls of the Catholic Association, the Orange Order, Ribbon lodges, and the landlord interest. NPCs should have opinions on these groups that reflect their social position, religion, and personal history. The **parish priest** is a natural quest-giver and authority figure whose political role — collecting the Catholic rent, urging tenants to defy their landlord at the polls — creates dramatic tension with the landlord and his agent. **Election events**, whether a county election or the distant thunder of Clare in 1828, can serve as set-piece moments that force the player to navigate competing loyalties. The **memory of 1798** can surface through older NPCs' stories, hidden pikes in thatched roofs, and the lingering fear that speaking too freely about rebellion might bring the authorities to one's door. These layers of political tension — constitutional versus revolutionary, Catholic versus Protestant, tenant versus landlord, local versus national — provide a web of conflict and choice that can animate every interaction in the parish.
