# Farming & Agriculture in 1820s Ireland

## Research Document — Parish (Kilteevan, County Roscommon)

### The Land and Its People

In the 1820s, the Irish countryside was a patchwork of subsistence farming, communal tradition, and mounting pressure from an increasingly commercialised landlord class. County Roscommon sat in the **rich midlands**, a region of relatively fertile limestone soils that could support both tillage and grazing, in contrast to the **poor western bogland** of Connacht's Atlantic fringe where thin acidic soil over blanket bog made cultivation a grinding labour. Kilteevan's position gave its tenants a marginal advantage — the land could grow oats and potatoes with reasonable reliability, and the pasture could fatten cattle — but this same fertility made it attractive to landlords seeking to consolidate holdings and convert tillage ground to profitable grazing. The tension between **tillage and pasture** defined much of the social conflict of the decade. Tenants needed tillage to feed their families; landlords and graziers wanted pasture to feed the export trade in beef and butter.

### The Rundale System

The dominant form of land organisation among the smallholding class was the **rundale system**, a communal open-field arrangement with deep roots in Gaelic Ireland. Under rundale, a cluster of families — often related by blood — held a townland in common. The **infield**, the better-quality ground closest to the settlement cluster, was divided into narrow strips and periodically redistributed among the families so that no household was permanently stuck with the worst soil. The **outfield**, rougher ground on the margins, served as common grazing for cattle and sheep. Bogland was shared for turf cutting. This system promoted a strong ethic of communal labour and mutual obligation, but it frustrated landlords and their agents, who saw it as inefficient, resistant to improvement, and an obstacle to the rationalisation of estates. Throughout the 1820s, **consolidation and enclosure pressures** mounted as landlords sought to break up rundale clusters, square off holdings into compact individual farms, and evict those deemed surplus to the land's carrying capacity. The process was slow, uneven, and bitterly contested; it would accelerate catastrophically in the Famine years, but in the 1820s it was already a source of anxiety and occasional violence.

### Spade Cultivation and the Loy

The defining tool of Irish small farming was not the plough but the **loy**, a heavy narrow-bladed spade specific to Irish agriculture. Most smallholders in the 1820s could not afford a plough team — a plough required horses or oxen, harness, and open runs of ground unsuited to the narrow strips of rundale — and so virtually all cultivation was done by **spade husbandry**. The loy had a long wooden shaft with a single footrest and a narrow iron blade, and it was used to turn heavy sod with remarkable efficiency. A skilled man with a loy could prepare ground that a plough could not reach: steep hillsides, boggy margins, and the narrow ridges of lazy beds. Spade cultivation was labour-intensive but well suited to the conditions of Irish smallholding, and it persisted long after agricultural improvers condemned it as backward.

### Lazy Beds and Crop Rotation

The principal method of potato and grain cultivation was the **lazy bed** (in Irish, *iomaire*), a raised ridge of soil typically four to six feet wide, separated by trenches that provided drainage. The farmer cut sod from the trenches and folded it over seed potatoes or grain laid on the surface, creating a warm, well-drained growing medium even on wet ground. Lazy beds were not lazy at all — they required enormous labour to construct — but they were superbly adapted to Ireland's wet climate and heavy soils. A rough form of **crop rotation** was practised: potatoes in the first year, oats or barley in the second, and then a fallow or return to potatoes. Some farmers grew flax for linen in the rotation, though this was more common in Ulster. Turnips and cabbages appeared in kitchen gardens but were not yet field crops on any scale. The overwhelming dominance of the potato in the diet meant that most available ground went to potato cultivation, with only a fraction reserved for oats (often grown to pay rent rather than to eat).

### Potato Varieties

Not all potatoes were equal. The **Apple potato** (also called the Irish Apple) was the prestige variety of the early nineteenth century — flavoursome, relatively reliable, and preferred at table by those who could afford to be choosy. The **Cup potato** was another older variety with a good reputation. But by the 1820s, the **Lumper** was rapidly displacing these older sorts among the poorest tenants. The Lumper was a coarse, watery, tasteless potato, but it had one overwhelming advantage: it produced enormous yields on poor ground with minimal manuring. A family on a tiny patch of conacre ground could grow enough Lumpers to survive the year. The spread of the Lumper was a symptom of deepening poverty — families were being pushed onto worse land and smaller plots, and they needed a variety that would produce bulk above all else. Agricultural commentators of the period already noted the danger of this monoculture dependency, though their warnings went unheeded.

### The Conacre System

Many of the poorest families did not hold land at all in any permanent sense but rented it season by season under the **conacre system**. A conacre tenant paid a lump sum — often at extortionate rates — for the use of a patch of ground for a single season, typically to grow potatoes. The landlord or middleman provided the ground already manured; the tenant supplied the labour and the seed. Conacre was a desperately precarious arrangement. The tenant had no security, no claim on the land beyond the current season, and no incentive to improve the soil. If the crop failed, he had no means to pay the rent and no reserve to fall back on. Yet for labourers and cottiers without any other access to land, conacre was the only path to subsistence.

### Livestock and the Domestic Economy

Cattle were central to the rural economy, but for most smallholders they were not a source of food. Cattle were kept to generate **rent money**. A family might raise one or two young cattle through the year and sell them at a fair to meet the half-yearly rent. The pig, meanwhile, was famously known as **"the gentleman who pays the rent"** — a single pig, fattened on potato scraps and buttermilk, could be sold to cover smaller obligations or provide a cash reserve. Nearly every cabin kept a pig, often sharing the living quarters with the family. **Poultry** — hens and ducks — provided eggs for eating and for sale, while **geese** were fattened for the Christmas and Michaelmas markets and their feathers saved for bedding. The **dairy economy** was significant in Roscommon: families with even one cow produced butter for sale, and butter was a major export commodity. Butter-making was women's work, and the quality of a household's butter — properly salted, well-worked, packed in firkins — could make the difference between solvency and ruin.

### The Seasonal Farming Calendar

The farming year followed a rhythm that structured every aspect of rural life. **Spring** (March to May) was the season of planting: potatoes were set in the lazy beds around St Patrick's Day (17 March), oats were sown as soon as the ground could be worked, and gardens were planted with cabbage and herbs. **Turf cutting** began in May and continued into June; families travelled to the bog to cut, foot, and stack turf for the year's fuel, a communal activity that brought neighbours together for weeks of hard outdoor work. **Summer** was a time of tending — weeding, watching the crops, milking, and butter-making — and also the season of **cattle fairs**, where livestock were bought and sold at market towns across the county. **Harvest** arrived in late August and September: oats were reaped with sickle and scythe, potatoes were dug with the loy, and the turf was carted home from the bog. The harvest was the most labour-intensive period of the year, and it was governed by the tradition of the **meitheal** (pronounced roughly "MEH-hal"), a system of communal labour in which neighbours gathered to help each other bring in the crop. The meitheal was reciprocal — you helped your neighbour today, and he helped you tomorrow — and it reinforced the bonds of community, kinship, and mutual obligation that held rural society together. **Winter** (November to February) was a quieter time: cattle were housed or moved to sheltered ground, pigs were slaughtered and salted, spinning and craft work occupied the long evenings, and the community turned inward toward the hearth, storytelling, and religious observance.

### Land Disputes and Social Tension

The 1820s were a period of rising tension over land. Landlords and their agents pushed for **consolidation**, combining small rundale strips into larger squared-off farms and evicting those who could not pay increased rents. The **enclosure** of common grazing land — the outfield, the bog, the mountain — deprived the poorest families of resources they had relied on for generations. Middlemen, who leased large tracts from landlords and sublet them at a profit, added another layer of extraction. Secret agrarian societies — **Ribbonmen**, **Rockites**, and others — resisted enclosure, eviction, and tithe collection through intimidation, cattle-maiming, and occasional assassination. The Roscommon countryside in the 1820s was not a peaceful arcadia; it was a landscape under pressure, where every field boundary was potentially contested and every rent day carried the threat of dispossession.

## Game Design Implications

The agricultural world of 1820s Kilteevan provides a rich foundation for the Parish simulation. **NPC daily routines** should reflect the seasonal calendar: farmers working lazy beds in spring, families at the bog in early summer, the bustle of harvest meitheal in autumn, and the quieter domestic rhythms of winter. The time of year should visibly change what NPCs are doing, where they are found, and what they talk about. **Cattle fairs** and **market days** offer natural set-piece encounters — crowded, social, charged with economic anxiety — where the player can overhear gossip, witness disputes, and interact with a wider cast of characters than the townland normally provides.

**Farming as backdrop for encounters** means that the player should regularly come across NPCs engaged in agricultural labour: a man turning lazy beds with his loy, women making butter, children minding geese, a meitheal gang bringing in the oats. These encounters ground the world in physical reality and provide natural openings for conversation and story. The **conacre system** and **rundale redistribution** offer potent sources of interpersonal conflict — who gets which strip, who is being squeezed out, who cannot pay — that can drive NPC relationships and quest lines without requiring any supernatural or melodramatic contrivance.

**Land disputes** between tenants and landlord agents, between neighbouring families over strip boundaries, and between the communal old order and the enclosing new one should be a persistent background tension that the player can engage with, take sides in, or simply observe. The **meitheal** system provides a mechanic for communal reputation: a player who helps at harvest earns goodwill; one who refuses or is absent is noticed and remembered. Seasonal events — the first potato setting, the turf-cutting season, the harvest home, the November cattle fair — can serve as calendar anchors that give the game year structure and forward momentum.
