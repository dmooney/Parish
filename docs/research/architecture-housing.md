# Architecture & Housing in 1820s Ireland

*Research document for Parish — an interactive fiction game set in 1820s rural Ireland.*

## The One-Room Cabin

The dwelling of the poorest class — the **cottiers**, landless **labourers**, and **spalpeens** who made up perhaps a third or more of the rural population — was the **one-room cabin**, a structure so basic that it could be raised in a day or two with the help of neighbours. The walls were built from whatever lay to hand: rough **fieldstone** gathered from the land, or **mud** mixed with straw and sometimes animal hair, packed into crude courses and left to harden. In County Roscommon and the midlands, where limestone was abundant, even the meanest cabin might have stone walls, though they were dry-laid without mortar and far from weatherproof. The roof was **thatched** with straw, rushes, or whatever could be scavenged, laid over a frame of rough timbers or bog-wood. The floor was beaten **earth**, sometimes improved by being mixed with ox-blood or lime to create a harder surface, though in the worst cabins it was simply dirt that turned to mud in wet weather.

The most striking feature of the one-room cabin to any outside observer was the absence of a **chimney**. Smoke from the central hearth — or from a fire set against one gable wall — escaped through a hole in the roof or simply seeped through the thatch, blackening everything inside and filling the space with a haze that burned the eyes. Windows, if they existed at all, were tiny openings without glass, stuffed with rags or straw against the cold. The single door was the main source of light and ventilation. Inside this dark, smoky space, an entire family lived, slept, cooked, and often shared their quarters with a pig, a few hens, and perhaps a cow, whose body heat was genuinely valued in winter. Bedding was straw or rushes spread on the floor, sometimes on a raised platform of stones, and the family slept together under whatever coverings they possessed. Travellers who recorded their impressions of these cabins invariably expressed shock, but the people who lived in them regarded the arrangement as normal and would have considered the suggestion that animals be housed separately as an impractical affectation.

## The Vernacular Farmhouse

A considerable step up from the one-room cabin was the **vernacular farmhouse** of the **strong farmer** — a man holding a secure lease on twenty, thirty, or fifty acres, paying his rent reliably, and maintaining a standard of comfort that set his family visibly apart from the labouring poor. The strong farmer's house had **two or three rooms**, typically arranged in a line: a large kitchen that served as the main living space, a bedroom or parlour to one side, and sometimes a smaller room or loft at the other end. The walls were built of **dressed stone**, properly mortared and sometimes lime-washed to a gleaming white that announced respectability from a distance. The roof was **thatched** by a skilled thatcher — a respected craftsman — using oat or wheat straw, with the ridge neatly trimmed and pinned with **scollops** (sharpened hazel rods). A proper **chimney** carried smoke away from the hearth, and the fireplace itself was a substantial structure with a **brace** (lintel stone) across the top, a **pot crane** swung over the fire, and **pot-hooks** at various heights for controlling heat.

The **half-door** is the most iconic feature of the Irish vernacular farmhouse, and in the 1820s it was ubiquitous across the countryside. Split horizontally, the bottom half could be kept closed to prevent **animals** — chickens, dogs, pigs — from wandering in, while the top half remained open to admit light and air and to allow the occupants to lean out and observe the world passing by. The half-door was a social instrument as much as a practical one: a woman standing at her half-door was available for conversation, visible to neighbours, and connected to the life of the townland without leaving her domestic sphere. **Windows** in a strong farmer's house were small but glazed, a genuine marker of status, since glass was expensive and taxed. The interior was lime-washed white, making the rooms brighter than the smoked-dark cabin of a labourer.

## Interior Furnishings

The interior of a strong farmer's kitchen was dominated by a few key pieces of furniture that carried enormous social weight. The **dresser** — a large, open-shelved piece standing against the wall opposite the hearth — was the pride of the household, displaying the family's **delph** (crockery), arranged with great care and kept spotlessly clean. The quality, quantity, and condition of a family's delph on the dresser was read by every visitor as an index of prosperity and respectability. A well-stocked dresser with matching plates and jugs announced a household in good order; a bare or chipped dresser told a different story.

The **settle bed** was a bench by day that folded or opened into a bed by night, a clever solution to the problem of limited space. **Creepie stools** — small, three-legged stools low to the ground — were the most common seating, though a strong farmer's house might also have a few proper chairs and a **súgán chair** with a seat woven from twisted straw rope. The **pot crane** over the hearth was an iron arm that swung in and out of the fire, holding the great iron **bastable** (pot-oven) or the three-legged **skillet pot** in which the potatoes were boiled. A **churn** for making butter stood near the door, and its use was surrounded by folk beliefs — a stranger who entered during churning and did not lend a hand was suspected of trying to steal the butter's **profit** (its goodness or luck) through fairy magic.

## The Hearth

The **hearth** was the sacred centre of Irish domestic life, and its significance went far beyond the merely practical. The fire was **never allowed to go out** if it could possibly be helped. At night, the coals were **raked** and covered with ashes to preserve them until morning — a ritual act as much as a practical one, since the continuous fire symbolised the continuity of the family and its fortune. To let the fire die was considered unlucky, even shameful. When a new house was occupied, fire was carried from the old hearth to the new one, and when a daughter married and went to her husband's home, she might carry a coal from her mother's fire. The hearth was where food was cooked, clothes were dried, stories were told, prayers were said, and neighbours gathered for the evening **cuaird** (visit). The best seat in the house — the **hob**, a stone or wooden seat built into the chimney corner — was reserved for the eldest member of the household or for a honoured guest. A stranger was always invited to "come to the fire," and the offer of a seat by the hearth was the fundamental gesture of Irish hospitality.

## The Clachan

In the west of Ireland and across much of **Connacht**, including parts of County Roscommon, the characteristic settlement pattern was not the isolated farmstead but the **clachan** — a loose cluster of cabins and small houses occupied by a group of related families. The clachan was intimately associated with the **rundale** system of farming, in which the arable land of the townland was divided into scattered strips allocated among the families by periodic redistribution, while pasture and bog were held in common. A clachan might contain ten or twenty households, all sharing a surname or connected by marriage, their cabins grouped together without any formal plan, surrounded by a patchwork of tiny fields and potato gardens. The social life of the clachan was intensely communal: families shared tools, labour, and gossip; children moved freely between houses; disputes were settled (or not) within the group; and the sense of collective identity was powerful. To outsiders, particularly English-speaking improving landlords and their agents, the clachan looked like a disorderly slum, and the 1820s saw the beginning of sustained efforts to break up clachans and consolidate their scattered strips into compact, individually held farms — a process that would accelerate catastrophically during and after the Famine.

## Building Materials

Ireland in the 1820s was an almost **treeless** landscape. Centuries of clearance for agriculture, fuel, and export had reduced the island's once-extensive forests to a few remnants in demesne woods and inaccessible valleys. The consequence for building was profound: **timber** was scarce and expensive, and the structural timbers in a peasant cabin — the roof couples, the ridge pole, the door frame — were often the most valuable components of the house, carefully salvaged and reused when a cabin was abandoned or rebuilt. In Roscommon and the limestone midlands, **fieldstone** was the dominant building material, gathered from the land during clearing and piled into walls that served double duty as field boundaries and building material. In areas where stone was less available, **mud** construction predominated: walls of stiff clay mixed with straw, built up in courses and allowed to dry between layers, or packed into a timber frame in the **wattle-and-daub** technique. **Turf** (peat) was used not only as fuel but as an insulating material, packed against walls or used to build internal partitions. The roof covering was almost universally **thatch** — oat straw in the east and midlands, rushes and reeds in wetter areas, and even heather or potato stalks in the poorest districts. In towns and on landlord estates, **slate** was beginning to replace thatch on more substantial buildings, brought by canal or cart from quarries in other counties, but for the vast majority of the rural population, thatch remained the only option.

## The Big House

Standing apart from the world of cabins and farmhouses — literally as well as figuratively — was the **Big House**, the landlord's residence. In Roscommon and across the Irish midlands, these were typically **Georgian** or **Palladian** houses built in the eighteenth century, with classical proportions, symmetrical facades, sash windows, and fanlighted doorways. Inside, the Big House contained a world utterly removed from the lives of the tenants who sustained it: rooms with **plasterwork** ceilings, imported **mahogany** furniture, libraries stocked with English books, drawing rooms with pianos and portraits, and dining rooms where silver and crystal were laid on linen tablecloths. **Servants** — often drawn from the local Catholic population — maintained the house, cooked elaborate meals, tended the gardens, and staffed the stables. The **demesne** surrounding the Big House was enclosed by a high wall, within which lay landscaped grounds, ornamental plantings, and a kitchen garden that supplied the house with vegetables, herbs, and fruit that the tenants beyond the wall could never afford. The Big House was simultaneously the economic engine of the parish — the source of employment, the seat of the agent who collected the rent — and a symbol of the colonial relationship that shaped every aspect of Irish life. Relations between the Big House and the surrounding community were complex: there might be genuine affection for a resident landlord who treated his tenants fairly, but there was always an awareness that the house and everything in it had been built on extracted rent.

## Town Buildings

The nearest market town — and for Kilteevan, that would be places like Roscommon town or Strokestown — contained a different architectural vocabulary. The **market house**, often an arcaded stone building standing in the centre of the main street, was where business was transacted on fair days and where the town's modest commercial life was concentrated. The **courthouse** was a more imposing structure, built to project the authority of the law, where the **petty sessions** were held and local disputes adjudicated by magistrates drawn from the landlord class. The **barracks** housed whatever military or police presence the authorities maintained — in the 1820s, this might be a detachment of the **Peace Preservation Force** or a small military garrison. The **Catholic chapel** was often a surprisingly modest structure, reflecting the legacy of the **Penal Laws** that had restricted Catholic church-building; many chapels of this period were plain, barn-like buildings, though by the 1820s some parishes were beginning to build more substantial churches as Catholic Emancipation approached. The **public house** — the pub — ranged from a prosperous establishment with a proper bar and snug to a simple cabin where an unlicensed widow sold poitín from a jug. The pub served as the informal centre of male social and political life, and its owner was often one of the most influential figures in the town.

## Outbuildings

Around any farmstead of substance stood a collection of **outbuildings** essential to the agricultural economy. The **haggard** (or stackyard) was an enclosed area where harvested grain was stacked in ricks to dry before threshing — its size and the number of its ricks were a visible measure of the farmer's prosperity. The **byre** housed cattle during winter, and the **pigsty** kept the family's pig — an animal of enormous economic importance, since its sale at market often paid the rent. The **lime kiln**, a small stone structure with a draw-hole at the bottom, was used to burn limestone into quicklime for fertilising fields and whitewashing buildings. A **turf rick** — a carefully stacked pile of dried peat — stood near every house, its size an index of the family's industry during the summer cutting season. In the poorest holdings, these outbuildings might be nothing more than rough shelters of stone and thatch; on a strong farmer's land, they were solidly built and well maintained.

## Regional Variations

Housing in 1820s Ireland was not uniform, and the regional differences reflected both the availability of materials and the relative prosperity of different areas. The **west** — Connacht, including western Roscommon — was characterised by **stone construction**, since the rocky landscape provided abundant building material even as it made farming difficult. In the **midlands**, where glacial drift overlay the bedrock, **mud** and **clay** construction was more common, and houses tended to be lower and thicker-walled to compensate for the weaker material. In the **east** and in areas closer to towns and improving landlords, buildings were more likely to show English or Scottish influences: better carpentry, **slate roofs** replacing thatch, and more regular plans. The towns themselves were moving toward **slate and stone** construction as thatch — a perpetual fire hazard in closely packed streets — was gradually replaced, though the process was far from complete in the 1820s. Throughout all regions, the fundamental distinction was between the one-room cabin of the poor and the multi-room farmhouse of the comfortable, a distinction that mapped almost exactly onto the social and economic hierarchy of rural Ireland.

## Game Design Implications

The architecture and housing of 1820s Ireland offer rich material for building Parish as a living, legible world. **Location descriptions** should immediately communicate social class through architectural detail: the smoky darkness and earthen floor of a cottier's cabin versus the lime-washed walls and dresser full of delph in a strong farmer's kitchen versus the plasterwork and mahogany of a Big House drawing room. Each building type functions naturally as a **world node** — a place where specific interactions, characters, and events are situated — and the physical differences between them provide the player with an intuitive visual grammar for understanding the social landscape.

**Interior detail** is especially important for scenes set inside homes. The hearth should be a persistent presence in every domestic scene — the fire's state (blazing, low, banked in ashes) can signal time of day and household mood. The half-door, the dresser, the settle bed, and the pot crane are not merely decorative details but interactive elements: a conversation at a half-door has a different social register than one conducted by the hearth; an NPC glancing at her dresser while speaking to a visitor is performing a class-coded behaviour that the game can leverage for characterisation. The clachan layout supports the game's social dynamics by placing related NPC households in close proximity, creating natural clusters of interaction and gossip.

**Class-based housing** gives the game a built-in system for communicating the social hierarchy without exposition. When the player moves from a cabin to a farmhouse to the Big House, the architecture itself tells the story of inequality. The contrast between the enclosed, walled demesne of the landlord and the open, communal space of the clachan encodes the political tensions of the period. Town buildings — the courthouse, the barracks, the chapel, the pub — serve as nodes for distinct types of gameplay: legal encounters, encounters with authority, religious and community life, and social gathering. Outbuildings like the haggard, the byre, and the lime kiln provide settings for labour-related interactions and encounters that ground the player in the agricultural economy. Regional variations in building materials and styles can be used as the game world expands beyond Roscommon, giving each area a distinct visual and descriptive identity that reinforces the sense of moving through a real and varied landscape.
