# Technology & Crafts in 1820s Ireland

The Ireland of the 1820s was a pre-industrial world. While factories in Manchester and Birmingham were transforming English society with steam power, mechanised spinning, and iron foundries, the Irish countryside relied on tools and techniques that had changed little in centuries. This was not backwardness in any simple sense — it was an economy of scarcity and adaptation, where every community sustained itself through a web of skilled trades and domestic crafts that turned local materials into the necessities of life. For a parish like Kilteevan in County Roscommon, deep in the midlands of Connacht, the gap between local reality and the industrial revolution unfolding across the Irish Sea was vast and largely irrelevant to daily existence. What mattered was the smith who could repair a ploughshare, the thatcher who could keep the rain out, and the woman at the spinning wheel who could turn raw wool into thread.

## The Blacksmith

The **blacksmith** was the most important craftsman in any rural community, and in many parishes the only one who worked with metal. His forge was a social centre second only to the chapel and the public house — a place where men gathered to talk while waiting for work to be done. The smith made and repaired nearly every iron tool in the parish: **ploughshares**, **spades**, **billhooks**, **sickles**, **chains**, **hinges**, **nails**, and the iron fittings for carts and gates. He shod horses and donkeys, repaired pots, and could turn his hand to virtually any job that required heating, hammering, and shaping metal. His tools were the **anvil**, the **bellows** (often worked by an apprentice boy), the **slack tub** for quenching, **tongs**, and an assortment of **hammers** and **swages** for shaping. Iron itself was expensive and imported, which meant the smith spent as much time repairing and reworking old metal as making new items. A broken spade was not discarded but brought to the forge, heated, and welded back together. The smith occupied a social position somewhere above the ordinary farmer — he was a man of skill and knowledge, often literate, and his services were paid for in cash, kind, or reciprocal labour. In Irish folklore the smith held a quasi-magical status, and this cultural weight persisted well into the nineteenth century.

## Coopering and Cart-Making

The **cooper** — the maker of barrels, casks, and tubs — was another essential tradesman, though not every parish had one. Barrels were needed for storing and transporting butter, salted meat, grain, and of course whiskey and beer. The cooper shaped **staves** from oak or ash, bent them with heat and moisture, and bound them with iron hoops to create watertight vessels. **Coopered churns** were used in every household that made butter. A related trade was **cart-making**, which required both woodworking and ironworking skills. The typical Irish farm cart of the 1820s was a simple but sturdy affair: a flat bed mounted on two solid wheels, drawn by a horse or donkey. The wheels themselves were often **block wheels** — solid discs of wood rather than the spoked wheels common in England — though spoked wheels were increasingly seen on better roads. The cart-maker, sometimes called a **wheelwright**, shaped felloes, fitted spokes, and worked with the smith to add the iron tyres that protected the wooden rims. In many communities the same man served as both carpenter and cart-maker, turning his skills to whatever was needed.

## Thatching

**Thatching** was one of the most visible and essential skilled trades in rural Ireland. Nearly every dwelling in a parish like Kilteevan was roofed with thatch — typically **oat straw**, **wheat straw**, or **rushes**, depending on what was locally available. In coastal areas, **marram grass** was sometimes used. The **thatcher** was a specialist, though many farmers could manage basic repairs themselves. A well-made thatch roof, properly maintained, could last fifteen to twenty years. The technique involved laying bundles of straw over a framework of rafters and purlins, securing each layer with **scollops** — pointed sticks of hazel or willow bent into a U-shape and driven through the thatch into the roof timbers beneath. The ridge was the most critical and difficult part, requiring careful overlapping and weighting, sometimes finished with a decorative pattern that was the thatcher's signature. Thatching was seasonal work, done after the harvest when straw was plentiful, and a skilled thatcher could command good wages moving from farm to farm through the autumn months.

## Spinning, Weaving, and Flax Processing

The **spinning wheel** was found in virtually every cottage in Ireland. Women spun wool from the family's sheep or flax grown on a small plot, producing thread that was either woven into cloth at home or sold to weavers and merchants. The **great wheel** (or walking wheel) was used for wool, while the smaller **flax wheel** (or Saxony wheel) handled the finer flax fibre. Weaving was done on **hand looms**, and while not every household had a loom, most townlands had at least one weaver who produced plain cloth — **frieze** (a coarse woollen cloth) and **linen** — for local use and sale. By the 1820s, however, this domestic textile economy was in serious decline, undercut by factory-produced cloth from England and Scotland that was cheaper than anything a hand-spinner could match.

**Flax processing** was a particularly labour-intensive sequence of steps. After harvesting, the flax was **retted** — soaked in bog pools or slow-moving streams for one to two weeks until the outer stalk rotted away, releasing the inner fibres. The stench of retting flax was notorious and the pools were kept well away from dwellings. After retting, the stalks were dried and then **scutched** — beaten with a wooden blade against a board to break away the remaining woody core. The cleaned fibres were then drawn through a **hackle**, a board set with rows of sharp iron pins, which separated and aligned them into fine, combable strands ready for spinning. Each stage required skill and judgement; poorly retted flax produced coarse, weak fibre, and over-retted flax fell apart entirely. In the linen districts of Ulster this was a refined industry, but in Connacht it remained a cottage-scale operation supplementing the family income.

## Turf Cutting

For most of rural Ireland, **turf** (peat) was the sole fuel for heating and cooking, and its cutting, drying, and transport consumed an enormous amount of labour every year. The **bog** was as important to the household economy as the potato garden. Turf cutting began in late spring and followed a strict sequence. The top layer of heather and vegetation was stripped away with a **breast slean** — a specially shaped spade with a narrow, angled blade and a wing on one side that allowed the cutter to slice a uniform sod. The **slean** (also spelled sleán or slane) was the essential tool, and its design varied by region. Sods were cut to a standard size and thrown up onto the bank, where others — often women and children — spread them out to begin drying. After initial drying, the sods were **footed**: stood upright in small pyramids of four or five to allow air to circulate around them. Weeks later, when further dried, they were built into larger **stacks** or **clamps** that shed rain while continuing to cure. Finally, the dried turf was **drawn home** — carted or carried by donkey in creels along bog roads to be stacked against the gable end of the house, where a well-built turf rick was a sign of a provident household. A family might burn through several thousand sods in a year, and the quality of the turf — whether it was dense black bog-oak fuel from the lower layers or light brown surface turf — determined how well the fire held through a winter night.

## Lime Burning

**Lime burning** was a small-scale industrial activity found wherever limestone was accessible, and County Roscommon, sitting on a great limestone plain, had it in abundance. Farmers built crude **lime kilns** — circular stone structures with an opening at the base for a fire — and loaded them with broken limestone and fuel (turf or sometimes coal). The kiln was fired for several days until the limestone calcined into **quickite**, which was then slaked with water to produce **slaked lime**. This had two primary uses: mixed into a wash, it produced the brilliant **whitewash** that covered the exterior and interior walls of every cabin, keeping surfaces clean, bright, and somewhat insect-resistant; spread on the fields, it improved acid soils and boosted crop yields. Lime burning was hard, hot, dangerous work — quicklime reacted violently with moisture and could cause severe burns — but it was essential agricultural practice, and the remains of small kilns dot the Roscommon landscape to this day.

## Illicit Distilling

The production of **poitín** (anglicised as poteen) — illicit spirit distilled from grain, potatoes, or sometimes sugar — was widespread throughout Connacht and deeply embedded in rural culture. A typical **poitín still** was a simple copper pot with a coiled copper tube (the **worm**) that served as a condenser, the whole apparatus small enough to be carried on a donkey's back and hidden in a ditch, a ruin, or a remote bog hollow at short notice. The mash was fermented in wooden tubs, heated over a turf fire, and the vapour condensed through the worm, which was immersed in a barrel of cold water or run through a stream. A skilled distiller could produce spirit of surprising quality and strength. The authorities waged a constant campaign against illicit distilling — **revenue police** and **gaugers** (excise officers) patrolled the countryside, and informers were rewarded — but communities closed ranks to protect their stills, and the penalties for discovery, though severe, did little to suppress the trade. Poitín was cheaper than legal whiskey, avoided the hated excise duty, and was regarded by most of the rural population not as a criminal product but as a natural right. It was consumed at every social occasion, used as medicine, and served as an informal currency.

## Other Trades and Crafts

The **nailer** was a specialist smith who produced nails by hand — cutting iron rod, heating it, hammering it to a point, and forming the head in a small die. Nails were essential for construction and repair, and before factory-made wire nails became cheap, the nailer's trade was a viable livelihood. The **tinker** or **travelling smith** — a member of the itinerant community later known as Travellers — moved from parish to parish mending tin vessels, pots, and pans. Tinkers were viewed with a mixture of reliance and suspicion; they provided an essential repair service but lived outside settled society and carried with them the ambiguous status of the permanent outsider.

**Rope-making** from rushes and straw was a domestic skill practised in most households. **Súgán** — straw rope twisted by hand — was used for everything from tying down thatch to making the seats of chairs (a **súgán chair** being the standard seating in most cabins). **Candle-making** was another household necessity. The poorest families relied on **rush lights** — the pith of a rush stripped and dipped in tallow or grease, held in a simple iron clip — which gave a feeble, smoky light that lasted perhaps twenty minutes. Better-off households used **tallow candles** moulded or dipped at home. Beeswax candles existed but were a luxury largely confined to the church and the gentry.

**Fishing** supplemented the diet wherever water was accessible. On the Atlantic coast, fishermen used **currachs** — light boats made of a wooden frame covered in tarred canvas or animal hides — to venture into open water with nets and lines. Inland, fish traps and weirs were set in rivers to catch salmon and trout, and angling with simple tackle was common along every stream and lake. In a landlocked parish like Kilteevan, the rivers and small lakes of the surrounding countryside provided freshwater fish that varied the monotonous potato diet, though fishing rights were often controlled by the landlord and their exercise by tenants was technically poaching.

**Water mills** for grinding grain were the closest thing to industrial machinery in most rural parishes. A typical **corn mill** consisted of two large millstones housed in a stone building beside a stream, turned by a waterwheel fed from a millrace diverted upstream. The **miller** was a significant local figure — often a middleman as well as a tradesman — who ground oats, wheat, and barley for a toll (typically one-sixteenth of the grain). Mills were expensive to build and maintain, and the right to operate one was often tied to the landlord's estate, making the miller a figure of some economic power.

## The Industrial Divide

The contrast between this world of hand tools, animal power, and local materials and the industrial revolution transforming England could hardly have been sharper. By the 1820s, English factories were producing iron, textiles, and consumer goods on a scale and at prices that Irish cottage industry could not approach. Steam engines drove mills and pumps; canal networks and early railways moved goods efficiently; factory discipline organised thousands of workers under one roof. Almost none of this had reached rural Connacht. The few industrial enterprises in Ireland — breweries in Dublin, linen mills in Belfast, a handful of cotton mills — were concentrated in the east and northeast. For the people of Kilteevan, technology meant the slean, the spinning wheel, the forge, and the hand loom. Their world was powered by muscle, wind, and water, and would remain so for decades to come.

## Game Design Implications

The craft economy of 1820s Ireland provides rich material for NPC occupations and player interactions. The **blacksmith** should be a named, prominent NPC with a forge that serves as a social gathering point — a place where the player can overhear news, commission repairs, and encounter other characters. The **thatcher**, **cooper**, **miller**, and **weaver** are all potential NPCs with distinct schedules tied to seasonal work patterns: the thatcher busy after harvest, the turf-cutter active in late spring, the miller busiest when grain comes in after the autumn. Each trade involves specific **tools** that can be described with authentic terminology — the slean, the hackle, the scollop, the worm of a still — grounding the world in material detail.

**Craft-based interactions** offer natural quest and dialogue hooks. A farmer needs a spade repaired before turf-cutting season. The thatcher cannot work because scollop rods are scarce. A poitín still must be hidden before the gauger arrives. The miller is accused of giving short measure. Flax is retting in the stream and someone's cattle have fouled the pool. These are not abstract economic activities but specific, physical problems rooted in the technology of the period.

The **travelling tinker** provides a mechanism for bringing outside news and goods into the parish, and the social tension around itinerant people creates opportunities for moral choices. The **illicit still** is a natural source of conflict between community loyalty and legal authority, and the physical details of distilling — the copper pot, the worm, the hidden location — make it vivid and interactive. Economic activities like **lime burning**, **turf drawing**, and **rope-making** can serve as seasonal background events that NPCs discuss and participate in, giving the world a sense of cyclical time and communal labour. The technological gap with industrial England should be felt indirectly — through the cheapness of imported factory cloth that is destroying local spinning, through the occasional appearance of a manufactured item that seems impossibly fine and uniform — rather than stated didactically.
