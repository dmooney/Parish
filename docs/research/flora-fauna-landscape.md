# Flora, Fauna & Natural Landscape of 1820s Ireland

## Research Document — Parish (Kilteevan, County Roscommon)

### The Bog: Ireland's Defining Terrain

No feature of the Irish landscape loomed larger in the daily life and imagination of the 1820s than the **bog**. County Roscommon and the surrounding midlands sat upon vast expanses of **raised bog**, dome-shaped formations that had accumulated over millennia, their surfaces carpeted in **sphagnum moss**, heather, and cotton grass. To the west and into the mountains lay the **blanket bogs**, thinner but sprawling across entire hillsides, draped over the contours of the land like a sodden quilt. Both types were essential to survival. Every spring and summer, families cut **turf** (or peat) from their allocated bogland, slicing it into neat sods with a **sleán** (a specialised spade), then stacking it to dry in small pyramids called **footings** before carting it home for winter fuel. Turf was the lifeblood of the hearth, and without it a family could not cook, warm themselves, or survive an Irish winter.

Yet the bog was far more than a fuel source. It was a repository of wonders and terrors alike. **Bog oak** — the blackened, iron-hard trunks of ancient trees preserved for thousands of years beneath the peat — surfaced regularly during turf-cutting, eerie reminders of a time when Ireland was cloaked in forest. Occasionally, cutters unearthed **bog butter**, packages of butter buried in the peat centuries or even millennia earlier, still waxy and pale, preserved by the acidic, oxygen-starved conditions. Whether these caches were ancient offerings or simply an old method of cold storage remained a matter of local debate and folklore. The bog was also treacherous. Soft ground could swallow a careless walker to the waist, and **bog holes** — pools of dark water concealed beneath a thin crust of vegetation — drowned cattle and occasionally people. At dusk, the bogs took on an unsettling beauty: mist rising from their surfaces, the occasional flicker of **will-o'-the-wisp** (marsh gas igniting), and the vast silence broken only by the call of a snipe or curlew.

### A Land Stripped Bare: Deforestation and Hedgerows

A traveller arriving in 1820s Roscommon would have been struck by how **bare** the landscape appeared. By this period, Ireland had been almost entirely **deforested**, with perhaps only **two percent** of the land retaining tree cover — a dramatic transformation from the densely wooded island the Normans had encountered centuries earlier. Centuries of clearance for agriculture, shipbuilding, charcoal production, and deliberate colonial policy had reduced the ancient forests of oak, ash, and elm to a few scattered remnants. What woodland existed in the 1820s was largely confined to **estate plantations**, where landlords cultivated stands of beech, lime, and imported species behind their demesne walls. The ordinary tenant farmer lived in a landscape of open fields and sky.

In place of forests, the countryside was defined by **hedgerows** and **stone walls**, forming the intricate patchwork of field boundaries that gave the Irish landscape its distinctive character. Hedgerows were far more than property markers. They were corridors of biodiversity, sheltering a rich community of plants, birds, and small mammals. In autumn, they offered a harvest to the poor: **blackberries** (fraochán in some dialects), **sloes** for flavouring poitín, **hazelnuts**, **crab apples**, and **elderberries**. Hedgerows of **whitethorn** (hawthorn) and **blackthorn** provided windbreaks for livestock, and their dense thorny growth made effective barriers. In a land with little timber, hedgerow cuttings and gorse (furze) were also burned for fuel and used to heat bread ovens. The **fairy thorn** — a lone hawthorn standing in a field — was left untouched by even the most practical farmer, for fear of disturbing the **sídhe**.

### Water: Loughs, Rivers, and Drumlins

County Roscommon's eastern boundary was defined by the **River Shannon**, Ireland's longest river, and the great expanse of **Lough Ree** that lay along it. Lough Ree was a world unto itself — dotted with islands, rich in fish, and wrapped in folklore. Its waters yielded **pike**, **perch**, and **bream**, and its shores were sites of early Christian monasteries and ancient crannógs. For the people of Kilteevan and the surrounding townlands, the Shannon and its tributary the **River Suck** (which formed Roscommon's western boundary) were vital arteries of **transport** and **trade**, carrying goods by boat and barge. **Salmon** and **trout** ran in both rivers and their smaller tributaries, and fishing rights — often jealously guarded by landlords — were a source of both sustenance and conflict.

The terrain of the midlands was shaped by **drumlins**, the smooth, elongated hills deposited by retreating glaciers, giving the landscape a gently rolling, undulating character. Between the drumlins lay wet hollows, small lakes, and rushy meadows that flooded in winter. This was not dramatic scenery by the standards of the western seaboard, but it possessed a quiet, layered beauty — green upon green, water glinting in every hollow, the sky enormous above the low hills.

### Wildlife: Creatures of Field, Bog, and Water

The fauna of 1820s Roscommon reflected a landscape shaped by centuries of human use. The **red fox** was ubiquitous, both a pest to poultry-keepers and a figure of admiration in folklore for its cunning. The **badger** inhabited setts in hedgerow banks and woodland edges, emerging at dusk to forage. Three species of **hare** existed in Ireland: the native **Irish hare** (a subspecies of the mountain hare, turning white in winter in some regions), along with the brown hare in limited areas. The **otter** thrived along the Shannon, Suck, and their tributaries, hunting fish in the clear waters. **Pine martens** survived in small numbers in remaining woodland, while the **stoat** — Ireland's only native mustelid besides the otter and marten — was a common sight darting along stone walls. **Red deer**, once widespread, had been reduced to scattered herds by 1820, found mainly in the wilder parts of Kerry and Wicklow, though occasional individuals lingered in remoter Connacht woodlands.

The **bird life** of the 1820s was notably richer than today's. The **corncrake**, whose rasping call from meadow grass is now almost extinct in Ireland, was then a common summer visitor, its voice a defining sound of the rural night. **Curlew** and **snipe** populated the boglands and wet meadows, their calls — the curlew's long, mournful whistle and the snipe's drumming display flight — forming the soundtrack of the countryside. **Skylarks** sang overhead in every field. **Ravens** were widespread, associated in folklore with battlefields and prophecy. **Eagles** — both golden and white-tailed — still survived in parts of the west and northwest, though persecution by farmers and gamekeepers was driving them toward the extinction that would come later in the century.

### Farming Animals and the Domestic Landscape

The farmyard was a cacophony of animal life. Nearly every family, no matter how poor, kept a **pig**, often sharing the cabin with it in the poorest households. The pig was the "gentleman who pays the rent" — fattened on scraps and potato peelings, then sold to pay the landlord. **Poultry** were universal: **chickens** for eggs and occasional meat, **geese** fattened for Michaelmas and Christmas markets, and **ducks** where there was a pond or stream nearby. **Cattle** were the principal measure of wealth for larger farmers; by the 1820s, native breeds were increasingly crossed with **shorthorn** stock imported from England to improve milk yields and beef quality. **Donkeys** were the poor man's beast of burden, hauling turf from the bog, seaweed from the coast, and produce to market. Only those with means kept **horses**, which served for riding, ploughing, and carting. **Sheep** were more common in mountain areas and on poorer land, their wool essential for domestic spinning and weaving.

### The Seasons and the Weather

The Irish landscape was a landscape in constant transformation through the seasons. **Spring** brought a slow greening — first the hedgerows hazed with new leaf, then the fields brightened as grass surged under lengthening days. Primroses, violets, and wild garlic carpeted shaded banks. **Summer** was lush and full, the meadows high with grass for mowing, the bogs alive with dragonflies and the scent of heather beginning to bloom. **Autumn** turned the drumlins gold and russet, the hedgerows heavy with fruit and nut, the stubble fields pale under vast skies. **Winter** was grey, waterlogged, and long — the fields sodden, the rivers swollen, the bogs impassable in places, and the world reduced to the circle of firelight in the cabin.

Through every season, the **weather** was the dominant fact of existence. Ireland's climate was **mild** — snow was rare in the lowlands, and hard frosts were infrequent — but it was relentlessly **wet**. Rain came in every form: soft mist, steady drizzle, driving sheets blown sideways by Atlantic winds, and sudden downpours that turned lanes into streams. A fine day was cause for celebration and commentary. The changeable sky — sun and cloud alternating within minutes — produced the extraordinary quality of **light** for which Ireland is known: sudden shafts of gold breaking through grey, double rainbows arching over drumlins, the western sky blazing copper at sunset before the clouds closed in again.

### Wild Plants and Their Uses

The hedgerows, bogs, and meadows provided a pharmacopoeia and larder for those who knew where to look. **Rushes** were among the most versatile plants: their pithy cores were dipped in fat to make **rush lights** (the poor household's candle), their stems woven into seats for chairs and into crude roofing, and their presence in a field indicated wet, poorly drained ground. **Nettles**, abundant in disturbed ground near habitations, were gathered in spring as a vegetable (rich in iron) and their fibres could be processed into a rough cloth. **Watercress** grew in clean streams and was a valued salad green. **Wild garlic** (ramsons) filled damp woodland floors in spring with its pungent scent and broad leaves. These wild foods supplemented a diet that was otherwise dangerously narrow, especially for the poorest families subsisting almost entirely on potatoes and buttermilk.

### Game Design Implications

The natural landscape of 1820s Roscommon offers rich material for Parish's world-building and mechanics. **Location descriptions** should reflect the specific character of each terrain type — the spongy give of bog underfoot, the dense tangle of hedgerows, the open sweep of drumlin country, the glint of water in every direction. **Seasonal atmosphere** is essential: the same location should feel and read differently in each season, from spring's vivid greening to winter's grey waterlogging, and the game clock should drive these shifts in description and mood. **Weather effects** should be pervasive and varied — not merely "it is raining" but the specific quality of rain (mist, drizzle, downpour, the miraculous dry spell) and its practical consequences for travel, visibility, and the player's comfort. **Animal encounters** can punctuate journeys: a fox crossing the path at dusk, a hare bolting from a hedgerow, the distant call of a corncrake at night, an otter surfacing in the river, geese honking alarm as the player approaches a farmyard. **Foraging** for hedgerow fruits, watercress, nettles, and wild garlic could serve as a minor but flavourful mechanic, especially for players exploring poverty and self-sufficiency. Above all, the **landscape itself should function as a character** — not a neutral backdrop but an active presence that shapes mood, constrains movement, offers beauty and danger, and anchors the player in the specific, unmistakable world of early nineteenth-century rural Ireland.
