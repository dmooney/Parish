# Clothing & Textiles in 1820s Ireland

*Research document for Parish — an interactive fiction game set in 1820s rural Ireland.*

## Clothing as Social Marker

Nothing revealed a person's place in the world more immediately than what they wore. In 1820s Ireland, clothing was the most visible and inescapable marker of social class, legible at a glance from across a field or down a boreen. A landlord or his wife, dressed in imported English cloth and fashionable cut, could not be mistaken for a strong farmer in his heavy coat and good boots, and neither could be confused with the barefoot cottier in his ragged frieze. The gradations were precise and universally understood. A man's coat, a woman's shawl, the presence or absence of shoes — these told you everything you needed to know before a word was spoken. Clothing was also one of the most closely guarded markers of respectability. A family might endure hunger in private, but appearing at Mass in decent clothes preserved their standing in the community's eyes. To be seen in rags was to be pitied; to be seen barefoot at church was to be marked as destitute.

## Men's Dress

The characteristic garment of the Irish working man was the **frieze coat**, a heavy, coarse overcoat made from undyed or dark-dyed wool that had been fulled and napped to produce a dense, water-resistant fabric. Frieze was the cloth of the poor and the middling alike — a tenant farmer wore frieze as readily as his labourer, though the farmer's coat was newer, better fitted, and perhaps dyed a darker shade. Beneath the coat, a man wore a **linen shirt** — often the single garment that touched his skin — and **breeches** of corduroy, wool, or heavy linen, cut to the knee and fastened with buttons or ties. Corduroy, imported and therefore more expensive, was a mark of modest prosperity; the poorest men wore breeches of rough woollen stuff or even patched and repatched remnants of older garments. Stockings, when worn at all, were knitted wool. On his head a man wore a **felt hat**, broad-brimmed and battered, or in the west and south a knitted cap. The hat was kept on indoors and out, removed only in church or in the presence of a social superior — and the manner of its removal was itself a social signal.

**Footwear** was the sharpest dividing line. The better-off wore **brogues**, a term that in this period meant rough, heavy shoes of untanned or lightly tanned leather, often made locally by a travelling cobbler. Brogues were practical rather than elegant — thick-soled, sometimes stuffed with straw for warmth, and punched with holes to let water drain out after crossing boggy ground. But a great many people, particularly labourers, cottiers, and their families, went **barefoot** for much or all of the year. This was not always a mark of absolute poverty. In summer, going barefoot was common even among those who owned shoes, which were saved for Mass, fairs, and winter cold. Children went barefoot almost universally. A traveller on the roads in summer would see more bare feet than shod ones, and the sight was so ordinary that it attracted no comment from those accustomed to it — though English visitors remarked on it with unfailing astonishment.

## Women's Dress

The most iconic element of Irish women's dress in this period was the **red petticoat** — not an undergarment but an outer skirt, dyed red with madder, that appeared in every traveller's account and every artist's sketch of rural Ireland. The red petticoat was worn over a **linen shift** (the basic undergarment, serving as both chemise and nightdress) and beneath a **bodice** or short jacket of wool or linen, often dark-coloured. A second petticoat, sometimes of a different colour, might be worn underneath for warmth. Over everything went a **shawl**, the garment that was rapidly becoming the defining piece of Irish women's dress in the 1820s. Shawls were practical, versatile, and expressive: wrapped around the shoulders for warmth, pulled over the head in rain, used to carry a baby on the hip, or draped with deliberate grace on the way to Mass. A woman's shawl announced her means — a fine wool or even a printed cotton shawl spoke of relative comfort, while a rough homespun rectangle spoke of poverty. Women also wore **headscarves** or kerchiefs tied under the chin, particularly for working outdoors, and like men they frequently went barefoot.

## The Cloak and its Passing

The older garment that the shawl was displacing was the **cloak**, known in Irish as the **brat**, a large hooded mantle of heavy wool, usually dark blue, black, or deep red, that had been the characteristic outer garment of Irish women for centuries. The cloak was a dramatic, enveloping thing — it covered a woman from head to ankle and gave her a striking silhouette that visitors found both picturesque and slightly mysterious. By the 1820s, the cloak was still common in rural areas, particularly in the south and west, and was especially associated with older women and with the poorer classes. Younger women and those with any pretension to fashion increasingly preferred the shawl, which was lighter, cheaper, and more adaptable. But the cloak had not yet vanished, and in many parishes the older women clung to it as a mark of dignity and tradition. A woman in a good cloak was a woman of substance, and the quality of the wool, the depth of the dye, and the condition of the garment all spoke volumes.

## Bánín and Homespun

Much of the cloth worn by ordinary people was **homespun** — produced in the home from locally grown flax or locally reared wool. The most distinctive homespun fabric was **bánín** (from *bán*, meaning white), an undyed, cream-coloured woollen cloth particularly associated with the west of Ireland and Connacht. Bánín had a soft, natural beauty and a warmth that made it ideal for the damp Irish climate, but its undyed state was also an indicator of poverty, since dyeing required materials and skill that not every household possessed. A man in a bánín jacket was almost certainly from the west, almost certainly poor, and almost certainly Irish-speaking. The fabric carried cultural meaning beyond its practical function — it was the cloth of the Gaelic world, of the cottier and the smallholder, and it distinguished its wearer from the middling farmer in his dyed frieze or the gentleman in his imported broadcloth.

## Linen: From Flax Field to Fabric

**Linen** was Ireland's great textile, and its production touched every corner of the country even though the industry was overwhelmingly concentrated in **Ulster**. The process began with the growing of **flax**, a slender blue-flowered plant that demanded good soil and careful tending. After pulling (flax was pulled rather than cut, to preserve the full length of the fibre), the stalks were **retted** — soaked in bog pools or slow streams for days or weeks until the outer bark rotted away, releasing the fibrous core. The stench of retting flax was one of the characteristic smells of the Irish countryside in late summer, and the retting pools were a familiar landmark. The retted stalks were then dried, **scutched** (beaten to separate the fibre from the woody core), and **hackled** (combed through iron-toothed boards to separate the fine long fibres from the shorter tow). The prepared fibre was spun into thread on a **spinning wheel** and woven into cloth on a loom. In Ulster, this entire process supported a vast domestic industry that fed the great linen markets of Belfast and Lisburn. In Connacht and the midlands, flax was grown and processed on a smaller scale, primarily for household use — every family needed linen for shirts, shifts, and bedding.

## Wool and the Spinning Wheel

If linen was Ireland's commercial textile, **wool** was its domestic one. Nearly every smallholding kept a few **sheep**, and the annual shearing produced wool that was washed, **carded** (combed between two boards studded with wire teeth to align the fibres), and spun into yarn. The **spinning wheel** was perhaps the single most ubiquitous piece of equipment in an Irish cottage, and spinning was **women's primary cash-earning activity** outside of agricultural labour. A woman who could spin well and steadily could sell her yarn to a local weaver or at the market, and the income — though small — was often the only money a cottier's wife handled directly. The wheel turned in every spare moment: while minding children, while watching a pot, while visiting neighbours. The sound of the wheel was the background hum of domestic life. The yarn produced was either woven at home on a simple loom or sold to a weaver who produced **frieze**, flannel, or blanket cloth for local sale.

## Dyes and Colour

Colour in clothing was achieved through **natural dyes** derived from local plants, lichens, and a few imported materials. **Weld** (a yellow-flowered plant) produced bright yellow. Various **lichens** scraped from rocks gave purples, russets, and reddish-browns — the preparation of lichen dyes was a skilled craft, often known to particular women in a community. **Alder bark** produced black or dark brown. **Woad** and its relatives gave blue, though by this period imported **indigo** — far more potent and reliable — had largely replaced native blue dyes among those who could afford it. **Madder**, the source of the famous red of the petticoats, was also imported, though some was grown locally. The palette of rural Irish clothing was therefore not the uniform grey that outsiders sometimes imagined but a range of earthy, warm, and sometimes vivid colours, constrained by cost and availability. Undyed cloth — the creams, greys, and browns of natural wool — predominated among the poorest, while the ability to wear well-dyed garments was another quiet marker of means.

## Sunday Best and Seasonal Dress

Almost everyone, however poor, aspired to own one set of **Sunday clothes** — a good coat, a decent pair of breeches, a clean shirt for men; a better petticoat, a good shawl, and perhaps shoes for women. These clothes were worn to **Mass**, to fairs, to weddings, wakes, and pattern days, and were otherwise carefully stored away. The contrast between a man's workday appearance and his Sunday appearance could be striking, and the effort of maintaining that one good set of clothes in the face of grinding poverty was a quiet act of defiance against destitution. **Seasonal variation** in dress was real but simple. In summer, clothing was lighter and fewer layers were worn; bare feet were universal among the lower classes and common even among the middling sort. In winter, every available garment was layered on — extra petticoats for women, the heavy frieze coat buttoned tight for men, and rough stockings or foot-wrappings for those who had them. The transition from shod to barefoot and back again was one of the small seasonal rhythms of rural life, as regular as the turning of the bog or the lighting of the Bealtaine fire.

## The Collapse of Cottage Textile Production

By the 1820s, the domestic textile economy that had sustained countless families was under severe and accelerating pressure. **English factory-produced cloth** — machine-spun, machine-woven, cheap, uniform, and available in quantity — was flooding Irish markets and undercutting the price of homespun. A woman who spent days spinning yarn and a weaver who spent days at his loom could not compete with the output of a Lancashire mill. The collapse was uneven: Ulster's linen industry, increasingly mechanised itself, survived and adapted, but the woollen cottage industry of the south and west was being devastated. Families that had relied on spinning and weaving for the cash portion of their income — the rent money, the money for salt and tobacco and the few necessities that could not be grown or made — found that income drying up. The irony was bitter: the cheap factory cloth that was destroying their livelihood was also the cloth they were increasingly forced to buy, since they could no longer afford the time to produce their own. This economic displacement was one of the quieter catastrophes of pre-Famine Ireland, less dramatic than eviction or hunger but contributing steadily to the vulnerability of the rural poor.

## Game Design Implications

Clothing and textiles offer rich material for grounding Parish in physical, social, and economic reality. **NPC appearance descriptions** should foreground clothing as the primary visual indicator of class: a barefoot woman in a patched bánín shawl and faded red petticoat reads instantly as poor, while a man in a well-fitted dyed frieze coat and good brogues signals a strong farmer. The presence or absence of shoes is the single quickest class marker and should appear prominently in character descriptions. **Seasonal wardrobe changes** can reinforce the passage of time — NPCs should go barefoot in summer and bundled in winter, swap cloaks for shawls as fashion shifts, and appear in their Sunday best on Mass days and fair days.

**Class identification** through clothing allows the player to make rapid social assessments without explicit exposition. A player arriving in a new parish can read the community's economic condition from the state of people's clothes: the proportion of barefoot to shod, the quality of cloth, the presence of imported fabrics versus homespun. The **textile economy** — spinning wheels, looms, flax fields, retting pools, the weaver's cottage — provides a backdrop of productive activity that populates the world with purposeful NPCs and plausible economic interactions. A player might buy cloth from a weaver, commission a pair of brogues from a cobbler, or hear women discussing the falling price of yarn. The ongoing **collapse of cottage industry** provides ambient economic tension: weavers complaining about cheap English cloth, spinners finding no buyers for their yarn, families anxiously calculating whether they can still make the rent. This slow-motion crisis is historically accurate and dramatically potent, foreshadowing the greater catastrophe to come.
